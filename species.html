Field Guide · Seven Species

The Sea Turtle
Species Compendium

Seven magnificent species, each uniquely adapted to their ocean habitat — and each critically in need of our protection.

🐢
Leatherback
Dermochelys coriacea
Vulnerable

The giant of the sea turtle world — the leatherback can weigh up to 900 kg and dive deeper than 1,200 meters. Unlike other sea turtles, it lacks a hard shell, instead bearing a flexible, leathery carapace reinforced by thousands of tiny bone fragments.

Up to 2.1m long Up to 900 kg Dives 1,200m
🌿
Green Turtle
Chelonia mydas
Endangered

Named not for its shell but for the green fat beneath it — a result of a strictly herbivorous diet of seagrasses and algae. Green turtles play a vital role in maintaining healthy seagrass meadows by grazing them like underwater lawnmowers.

Up to 1.5m long Up to 230 kg Herbivore
🦅
Hawksbill
Eretmochelys imbricata
Critically Endangered

Recognized by its narrow, pointed beak — perfect for extracting sponges from reef crevices. Hawksbills are crucial reef-health managers; by controlling sponge populations they allow corals to thrive. Their striking shell was historically used for "tortoiseshell" jewelry.

Up to 1.0m long Up to 80 kg Reef specialist
🪸
Loggerhead
Caretta caretta
Vulnerable

Famous for their enormous, powerful heads and jaws, loggerheads crush hard-shelled prey like conchs and horseshoe crabs. They are one of the most widespread sea turtle species, nesting across the Mediterranean, Atlantic, and Pacific.

Up to 1.2m long Up to 200 kg Carnivore
🏝️
Olive Ridley
Lepidochelys olivacea
Vulnerable

The most abundant sea turtle species — and the most social. Each year tens of thousands of females come ashore simultaneously in a phenomenon called an "arribada," carpeting beaches in a breathtaking spectacle of mass nesting behavior.

Up to 0.75m long Up to 50 kg Arribada nester
🌺
Flatback
Natator depressus
Data Deficient

Endemic to Australia's coastal waters, the flatback is the only sea turtle species found nowhere else on Earth. Its notably flat, upturned shell edges are an adaptation to shallow-water continental shelf habitats where it hunts soft corals and sea cucumbers.

Up to 1.0m long Up to 90 kg Australia only
